Early Life

Joan Crawford, born Lucille Fay LeSueur on March 23, 1904, in San Antonio, Texas, had a tumultuous early life. Her parents separated shortly after her birth, and tragically, her older sister passed away.

After her parents’ separation, Crawford’s mother remarried, and the family relocated to Lawton, Oklahoma. It was during this time that Crawford developed a deep interest in the performing arts. She would often watch shows at the Ramsey Opera House, where her stepfather worked, and this exposure fueled her passion for the stage and cinema.

Unfortunately, Crawford’s early years were not without hardship. Financial difficulties plagued her family, and as a result, her education suffered significantly. Eventually, she made the difficult decision to drop out of college, but her determination to pursue a career in entertainment remained steadfast.

Career Highlights

Joan Crawford’s career began in the 1920s when she signed a contract with MGM. Initially, she had smaller roles, but her ambition and determination led to bigger parts. Crawford worked on improving her dance skills and participated in competitions to increase her name recognition. She transitioned seamlessly into sound movies and became known for her roles in romantic dramas and sophisticated films.

One of her most notable films was “Mildred Pierce,” for which she won an Academy Award in 1946. The film, based on the novel by James M. Cain, tells the story of a woman’s journey from waitress to successful businesswoman. Crawford’s powerful performance in the lead role solidified her status as a Hollywood star, and she showcased her versatility as an actress in both dramatic and glamorous roles.

“Mildred Pierce” was a milestone in Joan Crawford’s career, showcasing her ability to captivate audiences with her emotion and depth. She embodied the complex character of Mildred Pierce, displaying strength and vulnerability in equal measure.

Despite her success, Crawford faced a decline in popularity in the late 1930s. In 1938, an article titled “Box Office Poison” labeled her as one of the actors whose films had consistently underperformed at the box office. Undeterred, Crawford persevered and continued to act in films, showcasing her talent and versatility in a variety of roles.

    Feud with Bette Davis and “Mommie Dearest”

    Joan Crawford and Bette Davis, both prominent actresses of their time, had a well-known feud that captivated the public’s attention. The rivalry between the two women was ignited when they both became involved with Franchot Tone, a fellow actor. In an attempt to push Davis out of the picture, Crawford married Tone, which only fueled the flames of their animosity.

    The feud between Joan Crawford and Bette Davis reached its peak during the filming of “What Ever Happened to Baby Jane?” in 1962, where they starred together. As tensions rose on set, the clash of their personalities became increasingly apparent, leading to intense conflicts and verbal sparring matches.

It was her daughter Christina Crawford who would ultimately shed light on their tumultuous dynamic. Christina wrote a memoir titled “Mommie Dearest,” where she depicted Joan Crawford as an abusive and controlling mother.

    Throughout her career, Joan Crawford had the opportunity to work alongside the top male stars of the time, creating memorable on-screen partnerships that captivated audiences.     Unhappy Endings and Retired Life

    Towards the end of the 1930s, Joan Crawford’s popularity began to decline, and she faced a challenging period in her career. She was labeled as “box office poison” due to a string of box office failures. However, Crawford continued to persevere and appeared in films throughout the 1940s and 1950s, even though finding substantial roles became increasingly difficult.

    One of the notable films of this period was the 1970 horror film “Trog,” which marked Crawford’s final on-screen appearance before retiring from acting. The film received negative reviews and failed to revive her career. After its release, Crawford decided to step away from the spotlight and lead a more private life.

    Crawford’s retirement was further solidified by a public appearance in 1974, during which unflattering photographs of her were published in the media. These images, coupled with her declining popularity, led Crawford to withdraw from public life and become increasingly reclusive.
